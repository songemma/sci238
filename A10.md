## **Problem 18.61**

_Suppose a neutron star with a mass of about $1.5 M_{\odot}$ and a radius of 10 kilometers suddenly appeared in your hometown. How thick a layer would Earth form as it wraps around the neutron star’s surface? Assume that the layer formed by Earth has the same average density as the neutron star._

(Hint: Consider the mass of Earth to be distributed in a spherical shell over the surface of the neutron star and then calculate the thickness of such a shell with the same mass as Earth. The volume of a spherical shell is approximately its surface area times its thickness: $V_{\text{shell}} = 4 \pi r^2 \cdot h$. Because the shell will be thin, you can assume its radius is the radius of the neutron star.)

---

### Solution:
**Known values:**
- $M_{\text{NS}} = 1.5 M_{\odot} = 3.0 \times 10^{30} \ \text{kg}$
- $r = 10 \ \text{km} = 1.0 \times 10^4 \ \text{m}$
- $M_{\text{Earth}} = 6.0 \times 10^{24} \ \text{kg}$

Assume both objects have the same density.

$\rho = \dfrac{M_{\text{NS}}}{\frac{4}{3} \pi r^3} = \dfrac{M_{\text{Earth}}}{4 \pi r^2 h}$
$h = \dfrac{M_{\text{Earth}} \cdot \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3}{4 \pi r^2 M_{\text{NS}}} = \dfrac{M_{\text{Earth}} \cdot r}{3 M_{\text{NS}}}$
$h = \dfrac{6.0 \times 10^{24} \cdot 1.0 \times 10^4}{3 \cdot 3.0 \times 10^{30}} = 6.6 \times 10^{-3} \ \text{m} = 6.6 \ \text{mm}$


**Final Answer:** $\boxed{6.6 \ \text{mm}}$

### **Problem 18.53:**

_You’ve just discovered another new x-ray binary, which we will call Hyp-X2 (“Hyp” for hypothetical). The system Hyp-X2 contains a bright, G2 main-sequence star orbiting an unseen companion. The separation of the stars is estimated to be 13 million kilometers, and the orbital period of the visible star is 5.2 days._

---

### **Part A:**

**Use Newton’s version of Kepler’s 3rd Law to calculate the total mass of the system.**

$$  
\text{Given:} \quad a = 1.3 \times 10^{10} \ \text{m}, \quad P = 5.2 \ \text{days} = 449280 \ \text{s}  
$$

Kepler's version:

$$  
\frac{a^3}{P^2} = \frac{G(M_1 + M_2)}{4\pi^2}  
\quad \Rightarrow \quad M_1 + M_2 = \frac{4\pi^2 a^3}{G P^2}  
$$

Plug in values:

$$  
M_1 + M_2 = \frac{4\pi^2 (1.3 \times 10^{10})^3}{6.67 \times 10^{-11} \cdot (449280)^2}  
= 6.4 \times 10^{30} \ \text{kg}  
$$

---

### **Part B:**

**Convert total mass to solar masses.**

$$  
M_{\odot} = 2.0 \times 10^{30} \ \text{kg}, \quad  
\frac{6.4 \times 10^{30}}{2.0 \times 10^{30}} = 3.2 \ M_{\odot}  
$$

---

### **Part C:**

**Determine the mass of the unseen companion.**  
The G2 star has $1.0 \ M_{\odot}$:

$$  
M_{\text{unseen}} = 3.2 - 1.0 = 2.2 \ M_{\odot}  
$$

---

**Final Answers:**

- Total mass: $6.4 \times 10^{30} \ \text{kg}$
    
- Total in solar masses: $3.2 \ M_{\odot}$
    
- Unseen mass: $2.2 \ M_{\odot}$

### **Problem Statement:**

_Models indicate that the gravitational-wave signal came from the merger of two black holes with masses of 25 $M_{\odot}$ and 33 $M_{\odot}$, forming a final black hole of mass 57 $M_{\odot}$. The missing mass was radiated away as gravitational waves._

---

### **Part A:**

Use $E = mc^2$ to calculate the energy released.

Mass lost:  
$$  
\Delta m = (25 + 33 - 57)\ M_{\odot} = 1\ M_{\odot}  
$$

Convert to kg:  
$$  
\Delta m = 1.99 \times 10^{30}\ \text{kg}  
$$

Calculate energy:  
$$  
E = (1.99 \times 10^{30}) \cdot (3.00 \times 10^8)^2 = 1.79 \times 10^{47}\ \text{J} \approx 1.8 \times 10^{47}\ \text{J}  
$$

---

### **Part B:**

Compare to supernova energy: $E_{\text{SN}} \approx 6 \times 10^{46}$ J

$$  
\frac{E_{\text{merger}}}{E_{\text{supernova}}} = \frac{1.8 \times 10^{47}}{6 \times 10^{46}} = 3.0  
$$

---

### **Part C:**

Compare to Sun’s total energy output over its main-sequence lifetime.

Sun’s lifetime:  
$$  
t = 10^{10}\ \text{years} = 3.15 \times 10^{17}\ \text{s}  
$$

Total energy:  
$$  
E_{\text{Sun total}} = (3.83 \times 10^{26}) \cdot (3.15 \times 10^{17}) \approx 1.2 \times 10^{44}\ \text{J}  
$$

Then:  
$$  
\frac{E_{\text{merger}}}{E_{\text{Sun total}}} = \frac{1.8 \times 10^{47}}{1.2 \times 10^{44}} = 1500  
$$

---

### **Final Answers:**

- $E_{\text{merger}} = 1.8 \times 10^{47}$ J
    
- $E_{\text{merger}} / E_{\text{supernova}} = 3.0$
    
- $E_{\text{merger}} / E_{\text{Sun total}} = 1500$
