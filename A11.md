## **Problem 19.57**

You are asked to use **Mathematical Insight 16.1**, which gives a way to estimate the **Jeans mass** — the minimum mass a gas cloud must have for **gravity to overcome pressure** and allow star formation.

The formula is:

$$
M \approx 18,000,M_{\odot} \left( \frac{T}{10,\text{K}} \right)^{3/2} \left( \frac{n}{1000,\text{cm}^{-3}} \right)^{-1/2}  
$$

Where:

- $T = 10^4\ \text{K}$
    
- $n = 3\ \text{cm}^{-3}$
    
- $M_{\odot}$ is the mass of the Sun
    

---

### **Step-by-Step Calculation**
$$
    M \approx 18,000,M_{\odot} \left( \frac{10^4}{10} \right)^{3/2} \left( \frac{3}{1000} \right)^{-1/2}  
    $$
$$
    \left( \frac{10^4}{10} \right)^{3/2} = (1000)^{3/2} = 10^{4.5} \approx 31,622.78  
    $$
$$  
    \left( \frac{3}{1000} \right)^{-1/2} = \left( 0.003 \right)^{-1/2} \approx 18.26  
    $$
$$
    M \approx 18,000 \times 31,622.78 \times 18.26 \approx 1.04 \times 10^7\ M_{\odot}  
    $$

## **Problem 19.59**

_The kinetic energy $E_{\text{kinetic}}$ of an amount of mass $M$ traveling at a velocity $v$ is given by the formula $E_{\text{kinetic}} = \frac{1}{2} M v^2$. The total kinetic energy of the matter ejected from a supernova explosion is about $10^{44}$ joules._

---

### **Part A**

**Determine the typical speed at which that matter is ejected from a supernova with a mass of $10 , M_{\odot}$.**

---

### **Solution:**

**Known values:**

- $E_{\text{kinetic}} = 10^{44} \ \text{J}$
    
- $M = 10 M_{\odot} = 10 \cdot 2.0 \times 10^{30} \ \text{kg} = 2.0 \times 10^{31} \ \text{kg}$
    

Using the kinetic energy formula:

$$  
E_{\text{kinetic}} = \frac{1}{2} M v^2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad v = \sqrt{\frac{2 E_{\text{kinetic}}}{M}}  
$$

$$  
v = \sqrt{ \frac{2 \cdot 10^{44}}{2.0 \times 10^{31}} } = \sqrt{10^{13}} = 3.2 \times 10^6 \ \text{m/s}  
$$

---

### **Final Answer:**

$$  
\boxed{3.2 \times 10^6 \ \text{m/s}}  
$$

## **Problem 20.51**

_Suppose you observe a star orbiting the galactic center at a speed of $1100 \ \text{km/s}$ in a circular orbit with a radius of 20 light-days. Calculate the mass of the object that the star is orbiting._

---
### **Part A**

**Calculate the mass of the central object.**  
_Express your answer in solar masses to two significant figures._
### **Solution:**

**Known values:**

- Orbital speed: $v = 1100 \ \text{km/s} = 1.1 \times 10^6 \ \text{m/s}$
    
- Radius: $r = 20 \ \text{light-days}$  
    $= 20 \cdot (24 \cdot 3600 \cdot 3.0 \times 10^8) \ \text{m} = 5.18 \times 10^{14} \ \text{m}$
    
- Gravitational constant: $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11} \ \text{m}^3 \text{kg}^{-1} \text{s}^{-2}$
    

Use the circular orbital velocity formula:

$$  
v^2 = \frac{G M}{r} \quad \Rightarrow \quad M = \frac{v^2 r}{G}  
$$

$$  
M = \frac{(1.1 \times 10^6)^2 \cdot (5.18 \times 10^{14})}{6.674 \times 10^{-11}} \approx 9.39 \times 10^{36} \ \text{kg}  
$$

Convert to solar masses:  
$$  
M_{\odot} = 2.0 \times 10^{30} \ \text{kg}  
\quad \Rightarrow \quad  
\frac{9.39 \times 10^{36}}{2.0 \times 10^{30}} \approx 4.7 \times 10^6 \ M_{\odot}  
$$

---

### **Final Answer:**

$$  
\boxed{4.7 \times 10^6 \ M_{\odot}}  
$$

## **Problem 20.52**

_You have obtained spectra for several galaxies and measured the observed wavelength of a hydrogen emission line with a rest wavelength of 656.3 nm. Your data:_

- Galaxy 1: $\lambda_{\text{obs}} = 659.3 \ \text{nm}$
    
- Galaxy 2: $\lambda_{\text{obs}} = 668.0 \ \text{nm}$
    
- Galaxy 3: $\lambda_{\text{obs}} = 679.5 \ \text{nm}$
    
- Rest wavelength: $\lambda_{\text{rest}} = 656.3 \ \text{nm}$
    

---

### **Part A – Redshift**

**Calculate the redshift $z$ for each galaxy.**  
Use the formula:  
$$  
z = \frac{\lambda_{\text{obs}} - \lambda_{\text{rest}}}{\lambda_{\text{rest}}}  
$$

**Calculations:**

- Galaxy 1: $z = \frac{659.3 - 656.3}{656.3} = 4.6 \times 10^{-3}$
    
- Galaxy 2: $z = \frac{668.0 - 656.3}{656.3} = 1.8 \times 10^{-2}$
    
- Galaxy 3: $z = \frac{679.5 - 656.3}{656.3} = 3.5 \times 10^{-2}$
    

---

### **Final Answers (Part A):**

$$  
z_{\text{galaxy 1}} = 4.6 \times 10^{-3}, \quad  
z_{\text{galaxy 2}} = 1.8 \times 10^{-2}, \quad  
z_{\text{galaxy 3}} = 3.5 \times 10^{-2}  
$$

---

### **Part B – Recessional Speed in km/s**

**Use:**  
$$  
v = zc \quad \text{where} \quad c = 3.0 \times 10^5 \ \text{km/s}  
$$

**Calculations:**

- Galaxy 1: $v = 4.6 \times 10^{-3} \cdot 3.0 \times 10^5 = 1370 \ \text{km/s}$
    
- Galaxy 2: $v = 1.8 \times 10^{-2} \cdot 3.0 \times 10^5 = 5350 \ \text{km/s}$
    
- Galaxy 3: $v = 3.5 \times 10^{-2} \cdot 3.0 \times 10^5 = 1.06 \times 10^4 \ \text{km/s}$
    

---

### **Final Answers (Part B):**

$$  
v_{\text{galaxy 1}} = 1370 \ \text{km/s}, \quad  
v_{\text{galaxy 2}} = 5350 \ \text{km/s}, \quad  
v_{\text{galaxy 3}} = 1.06 \times 10^4 \ \text{km/s}  
$$

---

### **Part C – Speed as Fraction of $c$**

Just reuse the redshift values:

$$  
v = zc \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac{v}{c} = z  
$$

---

### **Final Answers (Part C):**

$$

### **Final Answers (Part D):**

## **Problem 20.49**

_Suppose you observe a distant galaxy with a lookback time of 13 billion years._

---

### **Part A – Maximum Age of the Galaxy When Light Was Emitted**

**Question:**  
What was the **maximum possible age** of that galaxy when the light we are now observing from it began its journey to Earth?  
(Assume the galaxy was born **less than a billion years after the Big Bang**.)

---

### **Known values:**

- Age of the universe ≈ $14 \times 10^9$ years
    
- Lookback time = $13 \times 10^9$ years
The light left the galaxy **13 billion years ago**, so at that time, the universe was:  
$$  
14 - 13 = 1 \ \text{billion years old}  
$$

Since the galaxy formed **less than a billion years** after the Big Bang, the **maximum age** it could have at that time is:  
$$  
\text{Galaxy age} = 1 \times 10^9 \ \text{years}  
$$

---

### **Final Answer:**

$$  
\boxed{1 \times 10^9 \ \text{years}}  
$$
